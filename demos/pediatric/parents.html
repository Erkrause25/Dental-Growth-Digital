Dental Development

Dental Milestones by Age

Understanding what to expect as your child's teeth develop helps you provide the best care at every stage.

6-12 Months

First Teeth Emerge

Most babies get their first tooth around 6 months, usually a bottom front tooth. By their first birthday, they typically have 4-8 teeth.

Care Tips:
  • Wipe gums with a soft, damp cloth after feedings
  • Use a soft infant toothbrush when teeth appear
  • Schedule first dental visit by age 1
1-3 Years

Baby Teeth Fill In

By age 3, most children have all 20 baby teeth. This is when dental habits really start to matter.

Care Tips:
  • Brush twice daily with a rice-grain size of fluoride toothpaste
  • Begin weaning from bottles (especially at bedtime)
  • Limit sugary snacks and juice
3-6 Years

Preparing for Permanent Teeth

Baby teeth are placeholders for permanent teeth. Cavities in baby teeth can affect the health of adult teeth developing beneath.

Care Tips:
  • Increase to pea-sized amount of fluoride toothpaste
  • Start teaching proper brushing technique
  • Consider dental sealants when molars appear
6-12 Years

Mixed Dentition Period

Children lose baby teeth and gain permanent ones. First molars arrive around age 6, and most permanent teeth are in by age 12-13.

Care Tips:
  • Monitor brushingâ€”kids need supervision until age 7-8
  • Introduce flossing when teeth touch
  • Get orthodontic evaluation around age 7
12+ Years

Teen Dental Care

Most permanent teeth are in place. Wisdom teeth may start to develop in later teen years.

Care Tips:
  • Continue twice-daily brushing and daily flossing
  • Use mouthguards for sports
  • Avoid sugary drinks and energy drinks
All Ages

Regular Checkups Matter

Regardless of age, regular dental visits every 6 months are essential for maintaining healthy teeth and catching problems early.

What We Check:
  • Cavities and tooth decay
  • Gum health and bite alignment
  • Overall oral development

    Use the Right Amount Rice-grain size for under 3, pea-size for ages 3+
  2. 2
    Angle the Brush 45 degrees toward the gum line
  3. 3
    Use Gentle Circles Small, circular motionsâ€”not back and forth scrubbing
  4. 4
    Cover All Surfaces Front, back, and chewing surfaces of every tooth
  5. 5

  6. 6

Diet & Dental Health

What your child eats and drinks has a direct impact on their dental health.

Tooth-Friendly Foods

  • Water â€” The best drink for teeth! Rinses away food particles and keeps mouths hydrated.
  • Dairy Products â€” Cheese, milk, and yogurt are rich in calcium and help neutralize acids.
  • Crunchy Vegetables â€” Carrots, celery, and cucumbers help clean teeth naturally.
  • Lean Proteins â€” Eggs, fish, and chicken provide phosphorus for strong teeth.
  • Fresh Fruits â€” Apples and pears increase saliva production and are lower in acid than citrus.

  • Sugary Drinks â€” Soda, juice, sports drinks, and sweet tea bathe teeth in sugar and acid.

Common Questions

Parent FAQs

Answers to the questions we hear most often from parents.

When should my child start using fluoride toothpaste?

The American Academy of Pediatric Dentistry recommends using fluoride toothpaste as soon as teeth appear. For children under 3, use a rice-grain sized smear. For ages 3-6, use a pea-sized amount. Teach them to spit, not swallow.

My child sucks their thumb. Will it damage their teeth?

Thumb sucking is normal for infants and toddlers. However, if it continues past age 4-5, it can affect tooth alignment and jaw development. We can provide guidance and, if needed, recommend techniques to help break the habit.

Are dental X-rays safe for children?

Yes, dental X-rays are very safe. We use digital X-rays that emit up to 90% less radiation than traditional film X-rays. We also use lead aprons and only take X-rays when clinically necessary. The radiation from a dental X-ray is less than what you're exposed to during a day of normal activities.

What should I do if my child knocks out a tooth?

For a baby tooth: Don't try to put it back in. Clean the area, apply gentle pressure if bleeding, and call us. For a permanent tooth: Pick it up by the crown (not the root), rinse gently with water, and try to place it back in the socket or store it in milk. Call us immediatelyâ€”time is critical!

How can I help my child overcome dental anxiety?

Stay positive and avoid using scary words. Read books about dentist visits. Play "dentist" at home. Visit our office for a tour before the appointment. During visits, our team uses age-appropriate explanations and positive reinforcement. For severe anxiety, we offer sedation options.

Do baby teeth really matter if they're going to fall out?

Absolutely! Baby teeth hold space for permanent teethâ€”losing them early can cause crowding. They're also essential for eating, speaking, and self-esteem. Infections in baby teeth can damage the developing permanent teeth underneath. Good care now sets the stage for lifelong dental health.

Dental Emergencies

Know what to do if your child has a dental emergency. Quick action can save a tooth!

  • Knocked-out tooth: Keep moist, call immediately
  • Severe toothache: Rinse with warm water, call us
  • Broken tooth: Save any pieces, apply cold compress
  • Bitten tongue/lip: Clean gently, apply pressure
